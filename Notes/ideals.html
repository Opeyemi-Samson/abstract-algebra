§ 1

Rings — A Quick Recap

Before ideals, we need rings. A ring is a set R equipped with two operations — addition and multiplication — satisfying:

Definition — Ring

A ring (R, +, ·) is a set R with two binary operations such that (R, +) is an abelian group, multiplication is associative, and multiplication distributes over addition. If multiplication is also commutative, R is a commutative ring. If R has a multiplicative identity 1, it is a ring with unity.

Key Examples of Rings
  • ℤ — integers (commutative, with unity)
  • ℤₙ — integers mod n
  • ℚ, ℝ, ℂ — fields (every nonzero element is invertible)
  • ℝ[x] — polynomials with real coefficients
  • Mₙ(ℝ) — n×n real matrices (non-commutative)
  • ℤ[i] — Gaussian integers a + bi with a,b ∈ ℤ
The analogy to keep in mind: Ideals are to rings what normal subgroups are to groups. They are the "right" notion of substructure that allows you to form quotient rings, and they arise as kernels of ring homomorphisms.
§ 2

Definition of an Ideal

In a group, a normal subgroup is one closed under conjugation. In a ring, an ideal is a subgroup of (R, +) that is closed under multiplication from either side by ring elements. This absorption property is what makes ideals special.

Definition — Left, Right, and Two-Sided Ideal

Let R be a ring. A non-empty subset I ⊆ R is:

A left ideal if: (i) a, b ∈ I ⟹ a − b ∈ I (subgroup of addition) (ii) r ∈ R, a ∈ I ⟹ ra ∈ I (closed under left multiplication) A right ideal if (i) holds and: (ii') r ∈ R, a ∈ I ⟹ ar ∈ I (closed under right multiplication) A two-sided ideal (or simply ideal) if it is both left and right.

In a commutative ring, left = right = two-sided, so we simply say ideal.

The Absorption Property

The key condition is absorption: multiplying any ring element by something in I lands back in I. This is stronger than just being a subring — a subring is closed under multiplication of its own elements, but an ideal absorbs all of R.

Subring vs Ideal: ℤ is a subring of ℚ, but it is not an ideal of ℚ: take r = 1/2 ∈ ℚ and a = 1 ∈ ℤ. Then ra = 1/2 ∉ ℤ. The absorption condition fails.
§ 3

Trivial, Principal, and Generated Ideals

Trivial Ideals

Every ring R has two obvious ideals: {0} (the zero ideal) and R itself. These are called the trivial ideals. A ring with no ideals other than these is called a simple ring.

Principal Ideals

Definition — Principal Ideal

Let R be a commutative ring with unity and a ∈ R. The principal ideal generated by a is:

⟨a⟩ = aR = { ar : r ∈ R } This is the smallest ideal containing a.

Every element of ⟨a⟩ is a multiple of a. For example, in ℤ: ⟨n⟩ = nℤ = {…, −2n, −n, 0, n, 2n, …}.

Finitely Generated Ideals

⟨a₁, a₂, …, aₙ⟩ = { r₁a₁ + r₂a₂ + … + rₙaₙ : rᵢ ∈ R } The ideal generated by elements a₁, …, aₙ.
Definition — PID

A Principal Ideal Domain (PID) is an integral domain in which every ideal is principal (generated by a single element). Examples: ℤ, F[x] for any field F, ℤ[i].

Example — Ideals of ℤ

Every ideal of ℤ is of the form nℤ = ⟨n⟩ for some n ≥ 0. So ℤ is a PID. The ideals are {0} = ⟨0⟩, ℤ = ⟨1⟩, 2ℤ = ⟨2⟩, 3ℤ = ⟨3⟩, …

Note ⟨2⟩ + ⟨3⟩ = ⟨1⟩ = ℤ (since gcd(2,3) = 1) and ⟨2⟩ ∩ ⟨3⟩ = ⟨6⟩ (lcm(2,3) = 6).

Example — Non-Principal Ideal

In ℤ[x], the ideal I = ⟨2, x⟩ = {2f(x) + xg(x) : f,g ∈ ℤ[x]} is not principal. If I = ⟨h⟩, then h | 2 and h | x in ℤ[x], forcing h = ±1, but then I = ℤ[x], which it isn't (e.g. 1 ∉ I). So ℤ[x] is not a PID.

§ 4

Operations on Ideals

Given ideals I and J of a ring R, we can form new ideals by various operations:

Sum: I + J = { a + b : a ∈ I, b ∈ J } — smallest ideal containing I and J Intersection: I ∩ J — largest ideal contained in both Product: IJ = { Σ aᵢbᵢ : aᵢ ∈ I, bᵢ ∈ J } — ideal of all finite sums of products Power: Iⁿ = I·I·…·I (n times)
Theorem

If I, J are ideals of R, then I + J, I ∩ J, and IJ are all ideals of R. Moreover: IJ ⊆ I ∩ J ⊆ I, J ⊆ I + J.

Example — In ℤ

⟨m⟩ + ⟨n⟩ = ⟨gcd(m,n)⟩  ·  ⟨m⟩ ∩ ⟨n⟩ = ⟨lcm(m,n)⟩  ·  ⟨m⟩⟨n⟩ = ⟨mn⟩

Coprime Ideals

Definition — Coprime Ideals

Ideals I and J are coprime (or comaximal) if I + J = R.

Chinese Remainder Theorem (Ring Version)

If I₁, …, Iₙ are pairwise coprime ideals of a commutative ring R, then:

R / (I₁ ∩ … ∩ Iₙ) ≅ R/I₁ × … × R/Iₙ
§ 5

Prime and Maximal Ideals

Two special classes of ideals are central to ring theory and algebraic geometry.

Definition — Prime Ideal

A proper ideal P ⊊ R is prime if for all a, b ∈ R: ab ∈ P ⟹ a ∈ P or b ∈ P.

Definition — Maximal Ideal

A proper ideal M ⊊ R is maximal if there is no ideal I with M ⊊ I ⊊ R — it is not contained in any larger proper ideal.

Theorem — Characterisation via Quotient Rings

Let R be a commutative ring with unity and I a proper ideal. Then:

  • I is prime  ⟺  R/I is an integral domain
  • I is maximal  ⟺  R/I is a field

Consequently: every maximal ideal is prime.

Prime ⟺ R/I is integral domain:
R/I has no zero divisors ⟺ (a+I)(b+I) = 0+I ⟹ a+I = 0 or b+I = 0 ⟺ ab ∈ I ⟹ a ∈ I or b ∈ I ⟺ I is prime. ∎

Maximal ⟺ R/I is a field:
R/I is a field ⟺ every nonzero element of R/I is a unit ⟺ for every a ∉ I, there exists b with ab ≡ 1 (mod I), i.e. ab − 1 ∈ I ⟺ the ideal ⟨I, a⟩ = R for all a ∉ I ⟺ I is maximal. ∎
Example — Prime and Maximal Ideals in ℤ

The nonzero prime ideals of ℤ are ⟨p⟩ = pℤ for prime p. These are also maximal (ℤ/pℤ = ℤₚ is a field). The zero ideal ⟨0⟩ is prime (ℤ is an integral domain) but not maximal. In ℤ, prime = maximal for nonzero ideals.

Example — In ℝ[x]

⟨x² + 1⟩ is maximal in ℝ[x] because x² + 1 is irreducible over ℝ, and ℝ[x]/⟨x²+1⟩ ≅ ℂ (a field). Meanwhile ⟨x² − 1⟩ = ⟨(x−1)(x+1)⟩ is not prime since (x−1)(x+1) ∈ I but neither factor is.

Example — Prime but not Maximal

In ℤ[x], ⟨x⟩ is prime (ℤ[x]/⟨x⟩ ≅ ℤ, an integral domain) but not maximal (⟨x⟩ ⊊ ⟨x, 2⟩ ⊊ ℤ[x]).

§ 6

More Examples

Example — Ideals of ℤₙ

The ideals of ℤₙ are exactly ⟨d⟩ for each d | n. For ℤ₁₂: ideals are ⟨1⟩=ℤ₁₂, ⟨2⟩={0,2,4,6,8,10}, ⟨3⟩={0,3,6,9}, ⟨4⟩={0,4,8}, ⟨6⟩={0,6}, ⟨12⟩={0}.

Example — Matrix Rings

The ring M₂(ℝ) of 2×2 real matrices is a simple ring — its only two-sided ideals are {0} and M₂(ℝ) itself. This shows that in non-commutative rings, the theory of ideals is richer and more delicate.

Example — Polynomial Ideals and Geometry

In k[x, y] (polynomials in two variables), the ideal I = ⟨x, y⟩ consists of all polynomials with zero constant term. Geometrically, I corresponds to the origin (0, 0) — the variety where all polynomials in I vanish. This connection between ideals and geometric varieties is the heart of algebraic geometry.
