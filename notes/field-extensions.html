§ 1

Fields — A Recap

Definition — Field

A field is a commutative ring F with unity where every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse. Equivalently, F is a commutative ring in which the only ideals are {0} and F.

Examples of Fields
  • ℚ — rationals (smallest field of characteristic 0)
  • ℝ — reals
  • ℂ — complex numbers (algebraically closed)
  • ℤₚ = 𝔽ₚ — integers mod prime p (finite field, characteristic p)
  • 𝔽_{pⁿ} — finite fields with pⁿ elements (unique up to isomorphism)
  • ℚ(√2) — rationals adjoined √2

Every field F has a characteristic: the smallest n > 0 with n·1 = 0 (or 0 if no such n exists). The characteristic is always 0 or a prime p.

§ 2

Field Extensions

Definition — Field Extension

A field extension is a pair of fields F ⊆ E where F is a subfield of E. We write E/F (read "E over F") and call E an extension field of F, and F the base field.

Crucially: since F ⊆ E, we can view E as a vector space over F. The elements of E are vectors; the elements of F act as scalars via the field multiplication in E.

Examples
  • ℂ/ℝ — complex over reals. Basis: {1, i}. Dimension 2.
  • ℝ/ℚ — reals over rationals. Infinite-dimensional.
  • ℚ(√2)/ℚ — rationals adjoin √2. Basis: {1, √2}. Dimension 2.
  • ℚ(∛2)/ℚ — rationals adjoin ∛2. Basis: {1, ∛2, ∛4}. Dimension 3.
§ 3

The Degree of an Extension

Definition — Degree

The degree of a field extension E/F, written [E : F], is the dimension of E as a vector space over F.

[E : F] = dim_F(E) Finite extension: [E : F] < ∞ Infinite extension: [E : F] = ∞
Computing Degrees
  • [ℂ : ℝ] = 2   (basis {1, i})
  • [ℚ(√2) : ℚ] = 2   (basis {1, √2})
  • [ℚ(∛2) : ℚ] = 3   (basis {1, ∛2, ∛4})
  • [ℝ : ℚ] = ∞   (ℝ is not finitely generated over ℚ)
  • [𝔽_{p²} : 𝔽_p] = 2   (any degree-2 extension of 𝔽_p)
§ 4

Algebraic and Transcendental Elements

Definition — Algebraic Element

Let E/F be a field extension and α ∈ E. We say α is algebraic over F if α is a root of some nonzero polynomial f(x) ∈ F[x]. Otherwise α is transcendental over F.

α algebraic over F ⟺ ∃ f(x) ∈ F[x], f ≠ 0, with f(α) = 0 α transcendental over F ⟺ no such polynomial exists
Examples
  • √2 is algebraic over ℚ: root of x²−2
  • i is algebraic over ℝ: root of x²+1
  • ∛5 is algebraic over ℚ: root of x³−5
  • π is transcendental over ℚ (Lindemann, 1882)
  • e is transcendental over ℚ (Hermite, 1873)
  • Every element of 𝔽_q is algebraic over 𝔽_p (finite fields are algebraic)
Definition — Algebraic Extension

A field extension E/F is algebraic if every element of E is algebraic over F. Every finite extension is algebraic.

Suppose [E : F] = n and let α ∈ E. Consider the n+1 elements 1, α, α², …, αⁿ in E. Since dim_F(E) = n, these must be linearly dependent over F. So there exist a₀, a₁, …, aₙ ∈ F, not all zero, with a₀ + a₁α + … + aₙαⁿ = 0. This gives a nonzero polynomial f(x) = a₀ + a₁x + … + aₙxⁿ ∈ F[x] with f(α) = 0. So α is algebraic over F. ∎
§ 5

The Minimal Polynomial

Definition — Minimal Polynomial

Let α be algebraic over F. The minimal polynomial of α over F, written min_F(α) or irr(α, F), is the monic polynomial of least degree in F[x] having α as a root.

Theorem — Properties of the Minimal Polynomial

Let m(x) = min_F(α). Then:

  1. m(x) is unique and irreducible over F
  2. m(x) divides every polynomial in F[x] having α as a root
  3. F[x]/⟨m(x)⟩ ≅ F(α) — the simple extension generated by α
  4. [F(α) : F] = deg(m(x))
Consider the evaluation homomorphism ev_α : F[x] → E, f ↦ f(α). Its kernel is the set of all polynomials vanishing at α, which is an ideal I of F[x]. Since F[x] is a PID, I = ⟨m(x)⟩ for some generator m(x), which we choose monic. Since E is a field, Im(ev_α) = F[α] is an integral domain, so ⟨m(x)⟩ is a prime ideal of F[x], hence m(x) is irreducible (prime = irreducible in F[x]). By the First Isomorphism Theorem: F[x]/⟨m(x)⟩ ≅ F[α] = F(α) (since F(α) is the smallest field containing F and α, and it equals F[α] when α is algebraic). ∎
Computing Minimal Polynomials
  • min_ℚ(√2) = x²−2   (irreducible over ℚ by Eisenstein at p=2)
  • min_ℚ(∛2) = x³−2   (irreducible by Eisenstein at p=2)
  • min_ℚ(i) = x²+1   (irreducible over ℚ)
  • min_ℝ(i) = x²+1   (still irreducible over ℝ)
  • min_ℚ(√2+√3) = x⁴−10x²+1   (degree 4; splits into linear factors over ℚ(√2,√3))
§ 6

Constructing Extensions

Simple Extensions: F(α)

Given E/F and α ∈ E, the simple extension F(α) is the smallest subfield of E containing both F and α:

If α is algebraic with min poly m(x) of degree n: F(α) ≅ F[x]/⟨m(x)⟩ [F(α) : F] = n Basis: {1, α, α², …, αⁿ⁻¹} If α is transcendental: F(α) ≅ F(x) (field of rational functions) [F(α) : F] = ∞
Example — ℚ(√2)

min_ℚ(√2) = x²−2, degree 2. So ℚ(√2) = {a + b√2 : a,b ∈ ℚ} with [ℚ(√2):ℚ] = 2. Addition is componentwise; multiplication uses (√2)² = 2. The inverse of a+b√2 (nonzero) is (a−b√2)/(a²−2b²).

Example — ℚ(ζ₃) where ζ₃ = e^{2πi/3}

ζ₃ is a primitive cube root of unity, satisfying ζ₃³ = 1 and ζ₃² + ζ₃ + 1 = 0. The minimal polynomial is x²+x+1 (degree 2, irreducible over ℚ). So ℚ(ζ₃) = {a + bζ₃ : a,b ∈ ℚ} and [ℚ(ζ₃) : ℚ] = 2.

Splitting Fields

Definition — Splitting Field

A splitting field of f(x) ∈ F[x] over F is the smallest extension E/F over which f splits completely into linear factors:

f(x) = a(x − α₁)(x − α₂)···(x − αₙ) in E[x] E = F(α₁, α₂, …, αₙ) is the splitting field.
Theorem

Every polynomial f(x) ∈ F[x] has a splitting field over F, and any two splitting fields of f over F are isomorphic (though not necessarily equal).

Splitting Field Examples
  • Splitting field of x²−2 over ℚ is ℚ(√2) — both roots ±√2 live here.
  • Splitting field of x³−2 over ℚ is ℚ(∛2, ω) where ω = e^{2πi/3}. Degree 6 over ℚ.
  • Splitting field of xⁿ−1 over ℚ is ℚ(ζₙ) where ζₙ is a primitive nth root of unity (cyclotomic field).
  • ℂ is the splitting field of every polynomial over ℝ (and over any real closed field).

Algebraic Closure

Definition — Algebraically Closed / Algebraic Closure

A field F is algebraically closed if every nonconstant polynomial in F[x] has a root in F. The algebraic closure F̄ of F is the smallest algebraically closed field containing F.

Examples: ℂ is algebraically closed (Fundamental Theorem of Algebra). ℝ̄ = ℂ. The algebraic closure of ℚ is the field of all algebraic numbers, denoted Q̄ ⊂ ℂ.

§ 7

The Tower Law

The Tower Law is the fundamental counting tool for field extensions, analogous to Lagrange's theorem for groups.

Tower Law (Multiplicativity of Degree)

If F ⊆ K ⊆ E are fields (a "tower"), then:

[E : F] = [E : K] · [K : F]

In particular, [E : F] is finite iff both [E : K] and [K : F] are finite.

E
[E:K]
K
[K:F]
F
[E:F] = [E:K]·[K:F]
Let {αᵢ} be a basis of E over K and {βⱼ} a basis of K over F. Claim: {αᵢβⱼ} is a basis of E over F.

Spanning: Any e ∈ E writes as e = Σᵢ kᵢαᵢ with kᵢ ∈ K. Each kᵢ = Σⱼ fᵢⱼβⱼ with fᵢⱼ ∈ F. So e = Σᵢⱼ fᵢⱼ(αᵢβⱼ). ✓

Linear independence: If Σᵢⱼ fᵢⱼ(αᵢβⱼ) = 0 then Σᵢ(Σⱼ fᵢⱼβⱼ)αᵢ = 0. Since αᵢ are linearly independent over K: Σⱼ fᵢⱼβⱼ = 0 for all i. Since βⱼ are linearly independent over F: fᵢⱼ = 0 for all i,j. ✓

So dim_F(E) = |{αᵢβⱼ}| = [E:K]·[K:F]. ∎
Example — ℚ ⊆ ℚ(√2) ⊆ ℚ(√2, √3)

[ℚ(√2):ℚ] = 2. Now √3 ∉ ℚ(√2) (since √3/√2 = √(3/2) ∉ ℚ), so [ℚ(√2,√3):ℚ(√2)] = 2. By Tower Law: [ℚ(√2,√3):ℚ] = 2·2 = 4. Basis over ℚ: {1, √2, √3, √6}.

Example — ℚ ⊆ ℚ(∛2) ⊆ ℚ(∛2, ω)

[ℚ(∛2):ℚ] = 3. ω = e^{2πi/3} satisfies ω²+ω+1=0 so [ℚ(ω):ℚ]=2. Since ω ∉ ℚ(∛2) (real field), [ℚ(∛2,ω):ℚ(∛2)] = 2. Tower Law: [ℚ(∛2,ω):ℚ] = 3·2 = 6. This is the splitting field of x³−2, and its Galois group is S₃.

Degrees Divide

Corollary

If α is algebraic over F with [F(α):F] = n, and E/F is any extension with α ∈ E, then n | [E:F].

This is the field extension analogue of Lagrange's theorem: the degree of a subextension divides the degree of the full extension.

Application — Impossibility of Classical Constructions: Doubling the cube requires constructing ∛2. But [ℚ(∛2):ℚ] = 3. Any straightedge-and-compass construction produces extensions of degree a power of 2. Since 3 does not divide any power of 2, ∛2 cannot be so constructed. This proves the ancient problem is impossible.
